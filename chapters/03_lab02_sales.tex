\chapter{Lab 02 — Store Sales Forecasting}
\label{ch:lab02}

\section{Problem Definition}

\begin{problembox}
Forecast daily \texttt{sales} for each (date, store, product-family) combination over a \textbf{16-day horizon} — a classic \textbf{demand forecasting} task. Primary metric: \textbf{RMSLE} (Root Mean Squared Logarithmic Error), which penalizes relative error and is robust to the right-skewed sales distribution. The critical design constraint: \textbf{all lag/rolling features must be shifted at least 16 days} to prevent look-ahead bias.
\end{problembox}

\subsection{Dataset}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{6 CSV files:} \texttt{train.csv} (3,000,888 rows with labels), \texttt{test.csv} (28,512 rows, no \texttt{sales} — what we predict), \texttt{stores.csv} (metadata for 54 stores), \texttt{oil.csv} (daily oil price), \texttt{holidays\_events.csv}, \texttt{transactions.csv}.
  \item \textbf{Key columns:} \texttt{date}, \texttt{store\_nbr}, \texttt{family} (33 product categories), \texttt{sales} (target), \texttt{onpromotion} (items being promoted).
  \item \textbf{Time range:} approximately 5 years of training data (2013-01-01 through 2017-08-15); test covers 16 consecutive days (2017-08-16 through 2017-08-31) after the last train date.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Why lag features matter:} Sales data is fundamentally autoregressive — today's sales are the single best predictor of tomorrow's sales. Lag features operationalize this by creating shifted copies of the target variable aligned to specific temporal offsets:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{lag\_16}: captures the bi-weekly recurrence pattern. Since the forecast horizon itself is 16 days, the most recent observable sale is exactly at $t-16$. For products with stable bi-weekly inventory cycles, this is the strongest single predictor.
  \item \texttt{lag\_28}: captures the 4-week (lunar-month) cycle. Because 28 = 4 $\times$ 7, this aligns with the weekly calendar — a Monday 28 days ago is also a Monday, capturing the same day-of-week position one month back. This makes \texttt{lag\_28} the strongest baseline feature given the dominant weekly seasonality.
  \item \texttt{lag\_364}: captures the annual cycle (364 = 52 $\times$ 7, one year on the weekly calendar). Same-day-last-year sales encode holiday seasonality, back-to-school effects, and other yearly patterns that are invisible to shorter lags.
  \item \texttt{rolling\_mean\_7\_shift16}: 7-day rolling average of the 16-day-shifted window (covering $t-22$ through $t-16$). Smooths daily noise while preserving the recent trend direction.
  \item \texttt{rolling\_mean\_28\_shift16}: 28-day rolling average (covering $t-43$ through $t-16$). Captures the monthly trend — is this product family in an upward or downward trajectory?
  \item \texttt{rolling\_mean\_56\_shift16}: 56-day rolling average (covering $t-71$ through $t-16$). Provides the slow-moving quarterly trend, the most stable baseline for volatile product families.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{The shift-16 mechanism:} The forecast horizon is 16 days. On day 1 of the horizon, the most recent known sale is at $t-16$. Every lag and rolling feature must be shifted by at least 16 days relative to the prediction date. This is not an optimization detail — it is a hard constraint that separates a production-viable model from one that commits look-ahead bias:

\begin{pythoncode}[caption={Lag and rolling features with shift-16 guard}]
def add_lag_features(frame):
    out = frame.sort_values(["store_nbr", "family", "date"]).copy()
    grouped_sales = out.groupby(["store_nbr", "family"])["sales"]
    for lag in [16, 28, 364]:
        out[f"lag_{lag}"] = grouped_sales.shift(lag)
    out["rolling_mean_7_shift16"] = grouped_sales.transform(
        lambda s: s.shift(16).rolling(7, min_periods=1).mean()
    )
    out["rolling_mean_28_shift16"] = grouped_sales.transform(
        lambda s: s.shift(16).rolling(28, min_periods=1).mean()
    )
    out["rolling_mean_56_shift16"] = grouped_sales.transform(
        lambda s: s.shift(16).rolling(56, min_periods=1).mean()
    )
    return out
\end{pythoncode}

The \texttt{.shift(16)} applied to the raw sales column \textit{before} the \texttt{.rolling()} window is precisely what makes this pipeline production-ready. Without it, a hypothetical \texttt{lag\_1} feature would leak tomorrow's sales value at prediction time — trivial to implement, catastrophic in deployment. The \texttt{min\_periods=1} parameter ensures that even the earliest dates receive a rolling value (using whatever history is available), preventing NaN propagation at the start of each store–family time series.

\subsection{Time-Based Train/Validation Split}

Unlike standard machine learning where independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) assumptions permit random shuffling, time-series data exhibits strong temporal autocorrelation. Shuffling would place future observations in the training set, breaking the fundamental requirement that the model must predict \textit{forward in time} using only past information. The split is constructed with strict chronological ordering:

Only 365 days of the 5-year dataset are used for model fitting for three reasons: (1) the full 3,000,888-row dataset imposes prohibitive memory and compute demands on closed-form (Ridge) and iterative (MLP) algorithms; (2) sales patterns from 2013 may be economically stale — inflation, store openings, and consumer behavior shifts render very old data less relevant; (3) the maximum lag window is 364 days, so exactly one year of history provides full feature coverage with no information loss. A post-engineering Pearson correlation matrix confirms: \texttt{lag\_16} and \texttt{lag\_28} have the highest linear correlation with the target ($r > 0.600$); \texttt{onpromotion} is moderate ($r$ in 0.200–0.400); \texttt{dcoilwtico} is weak ($r < 0.200$) but statistically significant.

\subsection{Metrics and Preprocessing Pipelines}

\subsubsection{Why RMSLE over RMSE?}
Consider two predictions: Store A sells 100.000 units, predicted 110.000 (absolute error $= 10.000$, relative error $= 10.000\%$). Store B sells 10.000 units, predicted 20.000 (absolute error $= 10.000$, relative error $= 100.000\%$). RMSE penalizes both equally at 10.000, treating the absolute dollar error as the sole criterion. RMSLE penalizes the first at $\log(111.000) - \log(101.000) \approx 0.095$ and the second at $\log(21.000) - \log(11.000) \approx 0.647$ — a 6.8$\times$ difference reflecting the proportional impact on each store. In retail forecasting, a \$10.000 miss at a small convenience store is substantially more consequential than the same miss at a hypermarket. RMSLE is also robust to the zero-inflated, right-skewed sales distribution: the $\log_{1p}$ transform compresses extreme values and gives zero-sales observations finite, comparable errors.

\[
\text{RMSLE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\log(1+y_i) - \log(1+\hat{y}_i)\right)^2}
\]

\textbf{Two preprocessing pipelines} are constructed to match each model family's mathematical requirements:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{One-hot pipeline} — \texttt{MedianImputer $\rightarrow$ StandardScaler / OneHotEncoder}. For Ridge and MLP, where all features must be on a shared numerical scale. Median imputation handles missing values robustly (oil prices); StandardScaler centers to zero-mean unit-variance; OneHotEncoder converts 6 categorical features into 24 binary indicator columns.
  \item \textbf{Ordinal pipeline} — \texttt{MedianImputer / OrdinalEncoder}. For HistGradientBoosting, where tree splits depend on rank order. OrdinalEncoder maps each category to an integer, preserving the grouping structure without exploding the feature dimension.
\end{enumerate}

All models train on $\log_{1p}(\texttt{sales})$ to reduce target skew. Predictions are inverted with $\exp_{m1}(\hat{y}_{\log}) = e^{\hat{y}_{\log}} - 1$ to recover the original sales scale for metric computation.

\subsection{Train and Compare ML Models}

Three model families are implemented from scratch — no scikit-learn or XGBoost dependencies for the core algorithms:

\subsubsection{Ridge Regression — Closed-Form Solution}
The normal equation with L2 penalty: $\mathbf{w} = (X^TX + \alpha I)^{-1}X^T\mathbf{y}$. The intercept term is not penalized (the penalty matrix has a 0 in position $(0,0)$), ensuring that the overall bias term remains unconstrained while coefficients are shrunk toward zero. Advantages: deterministic solution (no convergence tuning, no random seeds), interpretable coefficients (each weight directly measures the linear contribution of one feature), and millisecond-level training on 648,648 rows. Limitation: can only learn linear relationships — an interaction like ``promotions work better on weekends'' requires explicit feature engineering rather than being discovered automatically.

\begin{pythoncode}[caption={Ridge Regression: closed-form normal equation with intercept protection}]
if self.fit_intercept:
    x_design = np.c_[np.ones(n_samples), x_np]
    penalty = self.alpha * np.eye(n_features + 1)
    penalty[0, 0] = 0.0    # do not penalize intercept
else:
    x_design = x_np
    penalty = self.alpha * np.eye(n_features)
A = x_design.T @ x_design + penalty
weights = np.linalg.pinv(A) @ x_design.T @ y_np.reshape(-1, 1)
\end{pythoncode}

\subsubsection{Multi-Layer Perceptron — Mini-Batch SGD}
A single-hidden-layer network with ReLU activation, trained via mini-batch stochastic gradient descent. He initialization ($W \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sqrt{2/\text{fan\_in}})$) is chosen over Xavier initialization because ReLU zeros out half the activations, requiring a factor of $\sqrt{2}$ compensation to maintain stable variance through the forward pass. Mini-batch training (batch size typically 64–256) provides a practical compromise: full-batch gradient descent is too expensive per iteration on 648,648 rows, while pure stochastic (batch size 1) produces high-variance gradient estimates that slow convergence.

\begin{pythoncode}[caption={MLP: forward pass, MSE loss, backward pass with ReLU derivative}]
# Forward
Z1 = X_batch @ self.W1 + self.b1
A1 = self._relu(Z1)
y_pred = A1 @ self.W2 + self.b2
loss = np.mean((y_pred - y_batch) ** 2)

# Backward
dZ2 = y_pred - y_batch
dW2 = (A1.T @ dZ2) / batch_len
dA1 = dZ2 @ self.W2.T
dZ1 = dA1 * self._relu_deriv(Z1)
dW1 = (X_batch.T @ dZ1) / batch_len

# SGD update
self.W1 -= self.lr * dW1;  self.b1 -= self.lr * db1
self.W2 -= self.lr * dW2;  self.b2 -= self.lr * db2
\end{pythoncode}

\subsubsection{HistGradientBoosting — 160 Trees}
An ensemble of shallow decision trees built sequentially, where each tree is trained on the residuals (negative gradient of the loss) of the current ensemble. The histogram-based implementation discretizes continuous features into at most 256 bins, reducing split-finding complexity from $O(n \log n)$ per feature to $O(\text{bins})$, which is constant and independent of dataset size. With 160 boosting iterations, the model builds 160 trees, each contributing a small correction. The learning rate (shrinkage) controls how much each tree contributes — lower rates require more trees but produce smoother, better-generalizing ensembles. The final prediction is the sum of all 160 tree outputs.

\subsection{Model Improvements}

\textbf{Interaction Features:} Two engineered interaction terms — \texttt{onpromotion $\times$ lag\_28} and \texttt{is\_weekend $\times$ lag\_28} — are pre-multiplied columns added to the feature matrix. For linear models (Ridge), these explicit products are the only way to capture multiplicative relationships: does the promotion effect amplify when a product already has high baseline sales? Does the weekend boost compound when recent sales are trending upward? For tree models, interactions are technically learnable through multi-level splits (e.g., split on \texttt{lag\_28} then \texttt{onpromotion}), but pre-computing the interaction gives the tree a direct path to discover it, improving training efficiency and often final accuracy.

\textbf{Ensemble Blend (60\% HGB + 40\% Lag\_28):} The final model combines HistGradientBoosting's non-linear pattern recognition with Lag\_28's stable seasonal baseline via a weighted average:

\[
\hat{y}_{\text{ensemble}} = 0.600 \cdot \hat{y}_{\text{HGB}} + 0.400 \cdot \hat{y}_{\text{Lag\_28}}
\]

This blend works because the two components have \textbf{complementary error profiles}. HGB excels at detecting promotions, holiday spikes, and store–family interactions that deviate from the seasonal norm. However, on regular days with no special events, HGB can overfit to noise in the training data, producing small but systematic errors. Lag\_28, being parameter-free, makes no such mistakes — its errors come only from events it structurally cannot model (promotions, holidays). By blending, HGB corrects Lag\_28's blind spots, while Lag\_28 stabilizes HGB's predictions on ordinary days. The 60/40 weighting reflects greater trust in the ML model while retaining a seasonal anchor that prevents large individual errors. The result: the ensemble improves on both components simultaneously across MAE, RMSE, and R$^2$.

\textbf{Log-Transformed Features:} Right-skewed numerical features (\texttt{onpromotion}, \texttt{dcoilwtico}, all lag and rolling values) are $\log_{1p}$-transformed before scaling. Calendar and holiday features (already bounded integers) remain untransformed. The impact on model performance is substantial:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Ridge:} RMSLE improves from 0.545 (raw features) to 0.513 (log features), a delta of $-$0.032. Linear models are highly sensitive to skew because extreme values exert disproportionate leverage on coefficient estimates. Log-transforming pulls in the right tail, reducing the influence of outlier observations.
  \item \textbf{MLP:} RMSLE improves from 0.521 (raw features) to 0.480 (log features), a delta of $-$0.041. Gradient descent in skewed feature spaces produces elongated loss contours that slow convergence; log-transforming makes the optimization landscape more spherical.
  \item \textbf{HGB:} RMSLE unchanged at 0.464. Tree-based models are invariant to monotonic transformations — splitting on the raw value or its log produces identical partitions and identical predictions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Model Diagnostics}

Three diagnostic views provide insight beyond aggregate metrics:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Actual vs. Predicted scatter:} Points should cluster tightly around the identity line $y = x$. A funnel-shaped spread (increasing variance with increasing sales) indicates heteroscedasticity — the log transform mitigates this by compressing the scale. The residual histogram should be symmetric around zero with mean $\approx 0.000$; any systematic bias (e.g., mean residual $> 0$) signals the model is consistently over- or under-predicting across the dataset.
  \item \textbf{Error by time and store type:} If residuals grow monotonically across the 16 validation days, the model is missing a short-term trend or regime shift that began during the forecast window. If one store type (e.g., Type E, small format/convenience) has systematically larger errors, that category's sales pattern diverges from the patterns learned from larger stores — suggesting either a separate sub-model or additional store-type interaction features.
  \item \textbf{Feature importance:} For HistGradientBoosting, feature importance is measured by total impurity reduction across all splits using that feature, averaged over the 160 trees. The top-ranked features are consistently \texttt{lag\_16}, \texttt{lag\_28}, and \texttt{lag\_364} — confirming that autoregressive features dominate all others. Calendar features (\texttt{dayofweek}, \texttt{month}) rank mid-tier, capturing the rhythmic component that lags don't fully encode. Store and family identifiers rank below lags but above external features, indicating that cross-sectional differences are significant but secondary to temporal patterns. \texttt{dcoilwtico} and holiday features rank lowest individually; their value lies in correctly adjusting predictions on specific, economically or culturally significant dates.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Final Model and Submission}

The best model is retrained on all 365 recent days (648,648 fit rows + 28,512 validation rows = 677,160 rows), incorporating the formerly held-out validation period to maximize the training signal before test prediction. The full \texttt{train.csv} and \texttt{test.csv} are concatenated and passed through \texttt{build\_feature\_frame()}, ensuring that lag features are computed seamlessly across the boundary between historical and future dates — the shift-16 guard automatically prevents any leakage into the test period. Predictions on \texttt{test.csv} produce a submission dataframe with two columns (\texttt{id}, \texttt{sales}), sorted by \texttt{id} for Kaggle compatibility.

\textbf{Key findings from the results:}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{HGB is the strongest individual model} at RMSLE $= 0.464$, beating the Lag\_28 baseline by 0.163 (a 26.000\% relative improvement). Its tree-based architecture naturally handles non-linear relationships (promotions have diminishing returns, holiday effects interact with product categories) that linear models require explicit feature engineering to capture.
  \item \textbf{Lag\_28 has the best R$^2$} (0.938) among individual models despite its simplicity. This underscores a fundamental truth in time-series retail forecasting: the seasonal baseline explains the overwhelming majority of variance, and model complexity buys incremental improvements on the remaining fraction.
  \item \textbf{Ridge and MLP produce higher RMSE} (420.310 and 398.150 respectively) than Lag\_28 (311.250), despite lower RMSLE. This apparent contradiction is explained by RMSLE's log-scale computation: Lag\_28 achieves excellent average accuracy but makes a few very large errors on promotion/holiday days that inflate RMSE; Ridge and MLP smooth these large errors at the cost of slightly worse typical-day predictions.
  \item \textbf{The ensemble blend resolves this tension.} By combining HGB (which corrects Lag\_28's holiday/promotion blind spots) with Lag\_28 (which stabilizes HGB's predictions on normal days), the ensemble achieves the best MAE (77.830) and RMSE (283.250), while maintaining competitive RMSLE (0.483). The ensemble's R$^2 = 0.949$ is the highest of all models, confirming the complementary nature of the two components.
  \item \textbf{Log-transforming features benefits linear models disproportionately.} Ridge improves from 0.545 to 0.513 (delta $-$0.032); MLP improves from 0.521 to 0.480 (delta $-$0.041). HGB is invariant to this transformation by construction — its split-finding algorithm operates on rank order, not absolute values.
\end{itemize}

The 60/40 blend ratio balances model complexity against stability: the HGB component captures the 6.200\% of variance that the seasonal baseline misses, while Lag\_28 anchors predictions during regular periods where complex models risk overfitting. Lag features consistently dominate feature importance rankings — \texttt{lag\_16}, \texttt{lag\_28}, and \texttt{lag\_364} are the top three predictors across all tree-based analyses, confirming that in retail demand forecasting, the past is overwhelmingly the best predictor of the future.
